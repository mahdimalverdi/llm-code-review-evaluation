# Related Work

The closest work to this paper does not come from a single literature stream. Code-review automation studies how review comments can be generated or transformed. Benchmark work studies how review systems can be compared. Grounding work asks whether generated claims are supported by the available evidence. Human-centered and industrial studies ask whether feedback is useful in practice. Evaluator-validity work asks whether human or LLM-based judgments can be trusted. A reviewer-facing assistant needs all of these views at once when deciding what to do with a generated comment.

The gap we focus on is therefore a decision-level gap. Prior work has made strong progress on individual pieces: richer quality rubrics, hallucination checks, PR-level benchmarks, context-aware generation, industrial filters, data curation, human-in-the-loop studies, static-analysis hybrids, and LLM-as-a-Judge protocols. What is still less developed is a paired comparison of mitigation strategies on the same review instances, where we can see not only which errors are removed but also which useful comments are lost, which cases are escalated, and which costs are introduced.

Figure \ref{fig:related-work-coverage-map} summarizes this positioning as a coverage map. The literature streams reviewed in this section provide different evaluation signals: comment quality, grounding, benchmark realism, data validity, workflow value, mitigation evidence, and evaluator reliability. Our concern is how those signals combine when a review assistant must decide whether a generated comment should be shown, rewritten, suppressed, escalated, or treated only as internal evidence.

The rest of this section follows that decision pipeline: review purpose, metrics, context, data quality, usefulness, failure types, mitigation strategies, evaluator validity, and specialized evidence needs.

## Human Code Review and Review Automation Foundations

Modern code review is not only a defect-detection activity. Empirical studies show that review also supports maintainability, knowledge transfer, team awareness, shared ownership, alternative-solution discovery, accountability, and developer learning [@p37_sadowski2018_google_mcr; @p38_bacchelli2013_expectations_mcr; @p39_bosu2015_useful_reviews; @p51_davila2021_mcr_slr_taxonomy]. This matters for LLM-based review because a generated comment can be technically correct and still be a poor use of reviewer attention. The reverse is also possible: a comment may not lead directly to an accepted patch, but it may still surface a risk, teach a convention, or trigger useful discussion.

Pre-LLM review automation established many of the tasks that LLM-based systems later extended. CodeReviewer framed code review as a sequence-to-sequence problem and evaluated generated review comments and revised code [@p14_li2022_codereviewer]. Earlier automation work showed that review comments and revisions can be mined from review systems, but the mined comments are heterogeneous: some ask for changes, some explain decisions, some discuss design, and some are not directly actionable [@p52_tufano2021_automating_code_review_activities]. Later qualitative analysis also showed why aggregate metrics are risky: they can hide which review scenarios a technique handles well and where it fails [@p53_tufano2024_code_review_automation_strengths_weaknesses].

For our study, this foundation sets an important boundary. A useful evaluation cannot stop at whether a generated comment resembles a reference or flags a defect. It must also ask whether the comment supports review work. This is why our framework separates correctness from usefulness, actionability, workflow impact, and preservation of review value.

## Evaluation Metrics and Benchmarks for Generated Review Comments

A benchmark can rank systems, but a reviewer-facing assistant still has to make a comment-level decision. That difference matters because review comments are diverse, context-dependent, and often valid even when they differ from a single human reference. CodeReviewer already noted the limitations of BLEU-style metrics for review-comment generation [@p14_li2022_codereviewer]. DeepCRCEval advances the evaluation discussion by proposing review-specific dimensions rather than relying only on generic text similarity [@p01_lu2025_deepcrceval]. Curated-review and preference-data studies make the same point from another direction: review quality involves relevance, clarity, conciseness, civility, usefulness, and preference judgments that exact reference matching cannot capture [@p18_bensghaier2025_curated_reviews; @p30_weyssow2025_codeultrafeedback].

These rubrics are necessary for our work, but they are not enough on their own. A richer rubric can say that a comment is relevant, grounded, useful, or clear. It still leaves open what should happen when those dimensions disagree. A comment may be plausible but weakly grounded, grounded but low-value, useful but too vague to show as-is, or relevant but costly to verify. In a deployed review assistant, those are not only scoring cases; they are action cases.

Recent benchmarks have also moved toward more realistic review settings. SWE-PRBench and SWRBench evaluate review behavior at the pull-request or software-engineering-review level, connecting review comments to broader change context and issue coverage [@p04_kumar2026_swe_prbench; @p05_zeng2025_swrbench]. ContextCRBench emphasizes enriched semantic and code context for more realistic review evaluation [@p06_hu2025_contextcrbench]. CodeReviewQA adds diagnostic probes for review-related comprehension [@p17_lin2025_codereviewqa]. These benchmarks are valuable because they make evaluation less artificial and closer to real review work.

The remaining issue is operational. A benchmark score may indicate model ranking, issue coverage, contextual adequacy, or similarity to expected feedback. It does not directly tell us whether a concrete generated comment should reach a developer. We therefore treat benchmark and rubric results as evidence for a downstream decision process, not as the decision itself.

## Context, Grounding, and Reviewability

Many poor generated review comments are grounding failures. HalluJudge frames hallucinated code review comments as claims that are unsupported by the available diff or review context [@p02_tantithamthavorn2026_hallujudge]. Production-oriented work reports similar failures when language, framework, version, or surrounding-code context is missing [@p03_tantithamthavorn2026_rovodev; @p07_olewicki2024_revmate]. Grounding is therefore a core property of comment quality, but it also depends on where the evaluator draws the context boundary.

Several systems try to improve review generation by expanding or specializing that context. Retrieval-augmented approaches use historical examples, related code, or project-specific evidence [@p11_zhang2025_laura; @p20_hong2025_rag_reviewer]. Context-aware systems explore file-level or repository-level evidence and routing mechanisms [@p16_icoz2026_context_aware]. Specification-grounded review uses explicit or implicit specifications as grounding evidence [@p12_wang2025_sgcr]. Static-analysis-guided approaches use analyzer warnings as focused evidence for LLM-generated feedback [@p22_jaoua2025_static_analyzers; @p48_patcas2026_code_quality_issues]. These systems show that additional evidence can help when it is relevant, fresh, interpretable, and correctly integrated.

The caution is that more context is not automatically better. SWE-PRBench reports settings where additional context can degrade review performance [@p04_kumar2026_swe_prbench]. Prompting and context-aware studies show that summaries, call graphs, retrieval depth, and model capacity can change whether context helps or distracts [@p13_haider2024_prompting_finetuning; @p16_icoz2026_context_aware]. Reviewability research adds that the input change itself can be hard to review when it is large, scattered, poorly described, or lacks rationale [@p40_ram2018_reviewability]. Documentation-behavior consistency work further shows that retrieved context can be stale or contradictory when documentation and behavior diverge [@p49_lee2025_metamon].

For our framework, this means that context itself must be evaluated. Context availability is not the same as context quality, and context quality is not the same as context usability. A generated comment can be correct under one context boundary, unsupported under another, and impossible to judge under a third. That is why weakly grounded comments may require rewriting or escalation rather than a simple correct/incorrect label.

## Data Quality, Reference Quality, and Provenance

Training and evaluation data are not neutral ground truth. Human review comments mined from repositories can be vague, low-value, unclear, uncivil, redundant, irrelevant, or difficult to interpret outside the original discussion. Too Noisy To Learn and curated-review work show that cleaning or reformulating review data can improve downstream modeling, but also that cleaning can change intent or remove useful signals [@p08_liu2025_too_noisy; @p18_bensghaier2025_curated_reviews]. Earlier review-automation work had to make a related distinction between comments that request code changes and comments with other review purposes [@p52_tufano2021_automating_code_review_activities].

Reference quality also depends on provenance. Reviewer-experience-aware work suggests that reviewer history, ownership, and expertise affect the informativeness and applicability of comments [@p23_lin2026_reviewer_experience]. Preference datasets such as CodeUltraFeedback can scale quality feedback, but their labels still depend on the selected judge, preference dimensions, and labeling process [@p30_weyssow2025_codeultrafeedback]. Studies of shared ChatGPT conversations in GitHub issues and pull requests add a newer concern: AI-generated explanations can become part of collaboration artifacts and influence review discussion without necessarily being verified [@p42_wasserbaech2024_chatgpt_github].

These studies motivate a dataset-validity layer. If a reference comment is unclear, if it does not describe a review-relevant issue, if it is linked to the wrong code, if the later revision ignored it for reasons unrelated to quality, or if the reference itself was produced or shaped by an LLM, then the evaluation instance may not support a reliable judgment. In that case, calling the model output simply correct or incorrect confuses a model failure with a data or benchmark failure.

## Human-Centered Usefulness and Workflow Signals

User studies and industrial deployments show that acceptance is not the same as usefulness. RevMate reports live user-study signals such as perceived value, acceptance, reviewer time overhead, and downstream revisions [@p07_olewicki2024_revmate]. RovoDev and BitsAI-CR add production-oriented signals such as actionability gates, code resolution, pull-request cycle time, human-comment reduction, outdated comments, survey feedback, and data flywheels [@p03_tantithamthavorn2026_rovodev; @p10_sun2025_bitsai_cr]. These signals are valuable because they connect generated comments to developer behavior and workflow effects.

Recent work on human-AI review interaction makes the target even less reducible to a single score. Human-AI synergy studies compare human and AI-agent review in terms of feedback diversity, interaction patterns, suggestion adoption, and code-quality impact [@p26_zhong2026_human_ai_synergy]. Empirical analysis of code review agents in pull requests suggests that automation can produce low-signal interactions and may be associated with weaker merge or abandonment outcomes in some settings [@p27_chowdhury2026_industry_claims]. Human-centered design work argues that AI should support review rather than fully automate it, because review also provides knowledge transfer, team awareness, shared ownership, and accountability [@p28_heander2025_support_not_automation].

A technically correct but obvious comment may still be noise. A rejected comment may still surface a useful risk. An accepted comment may be low-value or may increase future maintenance cost. These cases are why usefulness must be measured separately from correctness and acceptance. For a mitigation strategy, the question is not only whether it removes bad comments; it is whether it preserves the comments that support review work.

## Problematic Comment Types

The same generated comment can be problematic for different reasons, and those reasons should not be collapsed into one label. Some failures are about truth and grounding: the comment claims something unsupported, assumes a condition that is false, points to the wrong location, or proposes a fix that does not apply [@p01_lu2025_deepcrceval; @p02_tantithamthavorn2026_hallujudge; @p19_nguyen2025_fine_grained_classification]. Other failures are about usefulness and actionability: the comment may be vague, too low-value, hard to act on, or backed by a weak rationale even when it notices a plausible issue [@p18_bensghaier2025_curated_reviews; @p25_yu2024_finetuning_acr; @p41_widyasari2025_explaining_explanations]. A third group is not a model-output failure at all. The reference or benchmark instance may be noisy, unclear, low-quality, or stripped of the context needed to recover the original review intent [@p08_liu2025_too_noisy; @p52_tufano2021_automating_code_review_activities; @p53_tufano2024_code_review_automation_strengths_weaknesses].

Specialized review settings make this separation even more important. Secure review has to reason about vulnerability category, location, exploitability, severity, false alarms, missed vulnerabilities, and repair correctness [@p21_peng2025_icodereviewer; @p46_zhou2025_vulnerability_repair]. Static-analysis-guided review can fail because a warning is misread, overstated, or converted into an invalid repair suggestion [@p22_jaoua2025_static_analyzers; @p48_patcas2026_code_quality_issues]. Non-functional review requires workload and benchmark evidence; without it, a performance comment may be unsupported or merely a premature optimization nitpick [@p50_peng2025_coffe].

Our taxonomy therefore separates failure types from evaluation dimensions. Actionability is a dimension; a non-actionable comment is a failure type. Grounding is a dimension; an unsupported claim is a failure type. It also separates generated-review failures, input or context failures, workflow failures, and evaluator failures. This separation matters because different failures call for different actions. A vague but relevant comment may need rewriting. A clearly hallucinated comment should be suppressed. A weakly grounded but potentially important security concern may need escalation. A biased judge decision should trigger evaluator calibration rather than a model-quality conclusion.

## Mitigation Strategies and Their Trade-offs

Mitigation is often described by where it happens in the pipeline, but location is only a rough guide. Some strategies try to improve the input before generation; others change how comments are produced or intervene after the comment exists. Data cleaning, reviewability checks, prompt routing, retrieval, specification grounding, static-analysis context, fine-tuning, reward-guided optimization, multi-agent generation, structured outputs, hallucination detection, relevance filters, critics, rewriting, suppression, and human escalation are all examples of such interventions [@p08_liu2025_too_noisy; @p11_zhang2025_laura; @p12_wang2025_sgcr; @p21_peng2025_icodereviewer; @p22_jaoua2025_static_analyzers; @p09_ren2025_hydra_reviewer; @p24_bensghaier2025_reward_models; @p25_yu2024_finetuning_acr; @p02_tantithamthavorn2026_hallujudge; @p03_tantithamthavorn2026_rovodev; @p10_sun2025_bitsai_cr; @p35_mcaleese2024_llm_critics]. What matters for our study is not only the location of the intervention, but the decision it changes.

Each intervention buys something and risks something. A context gate can avoid unreliable review on low-quality inputs, but it can also skip useful automation opportunities. Retrieval can improve grounding while increasing token cost, latency, stale-context risk, and attention load. Static-analysis context can focus the model, but tool warnings can be false, irrelevant, or misinterpreted. Post-generation filters can reduce bad comments while suppressing rare but useful feedback. Human escalation can improve safety, but it adds delay and reviewer overhead. Critic-assisted review can increase comprehensiveness, but may also introduce hallucinated bug reports or low-value nitpicks [@p35_mcaleese2024_llm_critics].

The under-measured quantity across these strategies is useful-feedback preservation. Many papers report better scores, lower hallucination, higher acceptance, or fewer low-quality comments. Fewer report the useful comments wrongly removed, the human-flagged issues no longer covered after filtering, or the value lost when automation reduces human discussion. A trade-off-aware evaluation should therefore report both error-reduction and preservation outcomes. The important counts are not only harmful comments removed, but also useful comments retained, useful comments wrongly suppressed, review coverage retained after filtering, valuable-but-not-accepted comments preserved, human escalation rate, and verification cost. In short, mitigation should be evaluated by what it removes, what it preserves, what it accidentally loses, and what it costs.

## LLM-as-a-Judge and Evaluator Validity

LLM-as-a-Judge methods are increasingly used to evaluate software-engineering artifacts. They can reduce manual evaluation cost, support scalable comparison, and produce structured judgments. Recent work also warns that LLM judges should be treated as measurement instruments rather than ground truth [@m04_zheng2023_llm_judge; @p29_wang2025_human_evaluators; @p31_jiang2025_codejudgebench; @p33_he2025_llmjudge_se; @p36_li2024_llms_as_judges].

This warning is especially important in code review evaluation. If an LLM judge filters comments, scores usefulness, compares systems, or decides whether a comment should be escalated, then judge sensitivity becomes part of the evaluation result. Prior work shows that judgments can depend on task type, prompt wording, response order, source model, answer verbosity, preprocessing, output format, and other biases [@p29_wang2025_human_evaluators; @p30_weyssow2025_codeultrafeedback; @p31_jiang2025_codejudgebench; @p32_zhao2026_bias_loop; @p33_he2025_llmjudge_se; @p36_li2024_llms_as_judges]. Without calibration, repeated-run checks, answer-rate reporting, order-swap tests, prompt perturbation, and human agreement, evaluation may replace weak lexical metrics with a different weak proxy.

Our framework therefore separates evaluator validity from comment quality. A generated comment can be useful while an evaluator fails to recognize it, and a harmful comment can be rated as plausible by an uncalibrated judge. Judge outputs are useful evidence, but they are not final truth. Evaluator failures are part of the evaluation problem rather than noise to ignore.

## Specialized Evaluation Sublayers

Some review tasks require evidence that a generic quality score cannot provide. Security review must distinguish false alarms from missed vulnerabilities and validate vulnerability type, location, exploitability, severity, and repair correctness [@p21_peng2025_icodereviewer; @p46_zhou2025_vulnerability_repair]. Static-analysis and code-quality review must check whether a warning was interpreted correctly and whether the proposed change resolves the issue without changing intended behavior [@p22_jaoua2025_static_analyzers; @p48_patcas2026_code_quality_issues]. Efficiency and performance comments need workload and benchmark evidence, because generic performance advice may be unsupported or harmful to maintainability [@p50_peng2025_coffe]. These sublayers are optional extensions of the general framework, but they are useful reminders that some review comments need task-specific evidence before they can be shown, rewritten, suppressed, or escalated.

## Summary of the Gap

The gap is not a missing benchmark, a missing metric, or a missing judge. The gap is the lack of a paired decision-level comparison: when several mitigation strategies face the same generated comment, which errors do they remove, which useful signals do they preserve, and what cost do they introduce?

Prior work gives us many of the ingredients for that comparison: rubrics, grounding checks, realistic benchmarks, production signals, context evidence, data-quality analysis, failure taxonomies, and scalable judges. What remains under-specified is how these signals combine when a review assistant must choose among show, suppress, rewrite, and escalate.

Our work addresses this gap with an operational taxonomy and a trade-off-aware evaluation framework for controlled empirical comparison on shared review instances. The intended outcome is not a single universal score or another benchmark leaderboard. It is a structured way to compare which errors are reduced, which useful signals are preserved or lost, what costs are introduced, and which comments should be shown, suppressed, rewritten, or escalated.
